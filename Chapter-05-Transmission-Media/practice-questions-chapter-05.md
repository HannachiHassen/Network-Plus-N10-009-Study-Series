# ✅ Answer Key & Explanations

## Single Answer — Q1 through Q35

---

**Q1. Answer: C — Cat5e**
> Cat5e (Category 5 Enhanced) supports up to 1 Gbps (1000Base-T) at 100 meters. It improved on Cat5 by specifying tighter standards for crosstalk and attenuation, making it the minimum standard for modern Gigabit Ethernet. Cat5 only reliably supports 100 Mbps. Cat6 supports 1 Gbps at 100m and 10 Gbps at 55m. Cat3 supports only 10 Mbps and is used for telephone wiring.

---

**Q2. Answer: C — Cat6a**
> Cat6a (Category 6 Augmented) supports 10 Gbps (10GBase-T) at the full 100-meter distance. It achieves this through improved shielding and a larger cable diameter that reduces alien crosstalk at 10G frequencies. Cat6 supports 10 Gbps but only up to 55 meters. Cat7 also supports 10 Gbps at 100m but uses GG45 or TERA connectors — not commonly used in standard enterprise environments.

---

**Q3. Answer: C — Coaxial**
> Coaxial cable (coax) has a single center conductor, surrounded by a dielectric insulator, a braided metallic shield, and an outer jacket. It was used in early Ethernet: 10Base2 (Thinnet, 185m) and 10Base5 (Thicknet, 500m). It is still widely used for cable TV (RG-6) and DOCSIS cable internet. UTP is twisted pair. STP adds shielding to twisted pair. Fiber uses light.

---

**Q4. Answer: D — 100 meters**
> The 100-meter (328-foot) maximum distance is the standard for all UTP copper Ethernet cabling (Cat5e, Cat6, Cat6a) in IEEE 802.3 specifications. This includes up to 90 meters of permanent horizontal cabling and 10 meters of patch cables. Beyond 100 meters, attenuation causes signal degradation. Fiber or a network switch/repeater must be used for longer runs.

---

**Q5. Answer: B — Multi-mode fiber**
> Multi-mode fiber (MMF) has a core diameter of 50 microns (OM2–OM5) or 62.5 microns (OM1). Its larger core allows multiple light paths (modes) to travel simultaneously. This causes modal dispersion — light rays arrive at slightly different times — limiting distance. MMF is designed for short distances within buildings and data centers (up to 400–550 meters for OM3/OM4 at 10G).

---

**Q6. Answer: B — Single-mode fiber**
> Single-mode fiber (SMF) has a tiny 8–10 micron core that allows only one light path (mode). Eliminating modal dispersion allows SMF to transmit signals over much longer distances — up to 10 km (10GBase-LR), 40 km (10GBase-ER), and beyond with optical amplifiers. SMF uses laser light sources (1310 nm or 1550 nm). It is more expensive than MMF but essential for WAN and inter-campus links.

---

**Q7. Answer: D — Plenum-rated cable (CMP)**
> Plenum spaces (air-handling areas above drop ceilings and below raised floors) are used for HVAC air circulation. Building and fire codes (NEC Article 800) require plenum-rated cable (CMP — Communications Multipurpose Cable, Plenum) in these spaces. Plenum cable uses fluoropolymer jackets that produce minimal toxic smoke if burned, preventing poisonous fumes from being circulated by the ventilation system. Standard PVC cable is prohibited in plenum spaces.

---

**Q8. Answer: C — RJ-45**
> RJ-45 is the standard connector for UTP and STP Ethernet cables. It is technically an 8P8C (8 Position, 8 Contact) modular connector. Despite the industry calling it "RJ-45," true RJ-45 is slightly different — the industry standard connector for Ethernet is technically 8P8C. LC, SC connectors are for fiber optic. RJ-11 is the smaller 6P2C connector used for telephone/POTS lines.

---

**Q9. Answer: D — LC**
> LC (Lucent Connector, or Local Connector) is a small form-factor fiber connector with a 1.25mm ferrule and a latch retention mechanism. It is the most common connector for SFP and SFP+ transceivers and high-density fiber patch panels because it is half the size of SC connectors. It always comes in duplex pairs (one for transmit, one for receive) in standard Ethernet applications.

---

**Q10. Answer: C — ST**
> ST (Straight Tip) connectors use a bayonet-style mount — insert and twist a quarter-turn to lock. They are larger than LC and SC connectors and are found in older multimode fiber installations (pre-2000). They are being phased out in favor of SC and LC. ST connectors have a round 2.5mm ferrule similar to SC but use the twist-lock mechanism instead of a push-pull latch.

---

**Q11. Answer: C — SC**
> SC (Subscriber Connector, or Standard Connector, or Square Connector) is a square-profile, push-pull fiber connector with a 2.5mm ferrule. It is larger than LC but more common in older enterprise fiber installations and some single-mode applications. SC is also always used in duplex pairs for full-duplex Ethernet. Its square shape and secure push-pull mechanism make it easy to use but it takes more panel space than LC.

---

**Q12. Answer: D — MPO / MTP**
> MPO (Multi-fiber Push On) and MTP (a registered trademark of US Conec that is an improved MPO) connectors house multiple fibers in a single rectangular ferrule — typically 12, 24, or more fibers. They are used in high-density data center pre-terminated trunk cables and are essential for 40G (using 4 × 10G lanes over 8 fibers) and 100G (using 10 × 10G or 4 × 25G lanes) applications.

---

**Q13. Answer: C — UTP is more susceptible to EMI**
> UTP (Unshielded Twisted Pair) has no metallic shield — it relies only on the twist of the pairs to cancel electromagnetic interference. In high-EMI environments (near motors, generators, fluorescent lighting), UTP is more vulnerable to interference. STP (Shielded Twisted Pair) adds a foil or braided shield around the pairs or the entire cable, providing significantly better EMI protection. STP is more expensive and harder to install (requires proper grounding).

---

**Q14. Answer: B — Crosstalk**
> Crosstalk is the induction of an unwanted signal from one wire pair into an adjacent pair. In UTP cables, the twist of each pair is carefully designed to minimize crosstalk — different twist rates per pair reduce coupling. Attenuation is signal loss over distance. EMI is external interference. Jitter is variation in packet arrival timing. Crosstalk is an internal cable problem.

---

**Q15. Answer: B — NEXT (Near-End Crosstalk)**
> NEXT (Near-End Crosstalk) occurs near the transmitting end of the cable — the strong transmitted signal bleeds onto an adjacent pair right at the source, where the transmitted signal is at its strongest. FEXT (Far-End Crosstalk) is measured at the opposite end of the cable from the transmitter. Alien crosstalk is interference between adjacent cables (not pairs within the same cable). Higher NEXT values = better cable (more isolation).

---

**Q16. Answer: D — Attenuation**
> Attenuation is the reduction in signal amplitude as it travels along a conductor. It is caused by the electrical resistance of the copper conductor, which converts some signal energy into heat. Longer cables = more attenuation. Higher frequencies also attenuate more quickly, which is why 10G has a shorter maximum distance than 1G on the same cable category. Amplifiers/repeaters are used to overcome attenuation on long runs.

---

**Q17. Answer: C — SFP**
> SFP (Small Form-factor Pluggable) is a hot-swappable transceiver module that supports speeds up to 1 Gbps. It interfaces network equipment (switches, routers) to fiber or copper cables. The "mini-GBIC" label refers to SFP being a smaller replacement for the older GBIC standard. SFP+ supports 10 Gbps. QSFP/QSFP+ supports 40 Gbps. GBIC is the older, larger predecessor to SFP.

---

**Q18. Answer: D — SFP+**
> SFP+ (Enhanced Small Form-factor Pluggable) supports 10 Gbps and is the same physical form factor as SFP, making it backward-compatible in most cases. It is used for 10GbE connections over fiber (using 10GBase-SR, LR, ER wavelengths) or Direct Attach Copper (DAC). SFP+ is the dominant transceiver standard for 10G switch ports, server NICs, and storage interconnects.

---

**Q19. Answer: C — QSFP / QSFP+**
> QSFP (Quad Small Form-factor Pluggable) and QSFP+ support 40 Gbps (4 × 10G channels). QSFP28 supports 100 Gbps (4 × 25G channels). They are used in high-speed data center spine-leaf switches, 40G/100G server uplinks, and high-performance storage networks. SFP = 1G. SFP+ = 10G. QSFP/QSFP+ = 40G. QSFP28 = 100G. GBIC is obsolete.

---

**Q20. Answer: B — 1 Gbps**
> Cat6 cable supports 1 Gbps (1000Base-T) at the full 100-meter distance. It exceeds Cat5e specifications for crosstalk and attenuation, providing a cleaner signal at Gigabit speeds. Cat6 can also support 10 Gbps, but only at reduced distance (55 meters). For 10G at full 100m, Cat6a is required.

---

**Q21. Answer: D — 55 meters**
> Cat6 can support 10 Gbps (10GBase-T) but only at distances up to 55 meters. Beyond 55 meters, the alien crosstalk at 10G frequencies becomes too high for reliable operation. Cat6a was designed specifically to address this limitation, achieving 10 Gbps at the full 100-meter run through improved shielding and reduced diameter variability. This distance limitation is a common exam question.

---

**Q22. Answer: D — Direct Attach Copper (DAC)**
> DAC (Direct Attach Copper) cables have SFP+, QSFP, or QSFP28 transceivers built into the ends of a twinaxial copper cable. They are the most cost-effective solution for very short distances (typically under 7 meters for passive DAC, up to 15 meters for active DAC) in data centers. No separate transceivers are needed. They support 10G, 40G, and 100G speeds and are significantly cheaper than fiber + discrete transceivers for short runs.

---

**Q23. Answer: B — 10Base-T**
> 10Base-T was the first Ethernet standard over twisted pair (T = twisted pair), supporting 10 Mbps over Cat3 or better cable at distances up to 100 meters. It was ratified in 1990 and replaced the coaxial 10Base2 and 10Base5 standards. 10Base2 = Thinnet coax. 100Base-TX = Fast Ethernet (100 Mbps). 1000Base-T = Gigabit Ethernet.

---

**Q24. Answer: C — 1000Base-T**
> 1000Base-T (Gigabit Ethernet over copper) uses all four wire pairs simultaneously for bidirectional transmission and supports 1 Gbps over Cat5e or better at 100 meters. 100Base-TX uses only 2 pairs at 100 Mbps. 10GBase-T uses Cat6a or Cat7 for 10 Gbps. 1000Base-SX uses multi-mode fiber (not copper).

---

**Q25. Answer: D — 10GBase-SR**
> 10GBase-SR (Short Range) supports 10 Gbps over OM3 multi-mode fiber up to 300 meters, and over OM4 up to 400 meters, using an 850 nm VCSEL laser. The "S" = short range, using MMF. 10GBase-LR uses single-mode fiber at 1310 nm for up to 10 km. 10GBase-ER uses SMF at 1550 nm for up to 40 km. 10GBase-T is copper-based.

---

**Q26. Answer: C — 10GBase-LR**
> 10GBase-LR (Long Range) supports 10 Gbps over single-mode fiber using a 1310 nm laser at distances up to 10 km. The "L" = long range, using SMF. 10GBase-SR is multi-mode short range. 10GBase-ER is extended range SMF (40 km, 1550 nm). 10GBase-ZR goes even further (80 km). For inter-campus connections (5–10 km), 10GBase-LR is the standard choice.

---

**Q27. Answer: A — Riser-rated (CMR)**
> Riser-rated (CMR — Communications Multipurpose Cable, Riser) cable is designed for vertical runs inside building walls and between floors. It uses a flame-retardant jacket that prevents fire from spreading vertically. It is NOT rated for plenum spaces. Plenum (CMP) rated cable can be used in both plenum AND riser applications (CMP > CMR in fire rating). Standard PVC cannot be used in riser or plenum spaces in most code-compliant installations.

---

**Q28. Answer: B — Less toxic smoke, more fire-resistant**
> Plenum cable uses fluoropolymer (PVDF or FEP) jacket materials that are: (1) self-extinguishing (harder to ignite), (2) produce significantly less smoke when burned, (3) produce less toxic fumes (standard PVC produces hydrogen chloride and other toxic gases when burned). Since plenum spaces circulate air throughout the building, toxic smoke from burning PVC could travel everywhere via the HVAC system, posing serious health risks. Plenum cable does NOT provide higher data speeds.

---

**Q29. Answer: D — OM3**
> OM3 is a 50-micron laser-optimized multi-mode fiber designed for use with 850 nm VCSEL lasers. OM3 supports 10GBase-SR at up to 300 meters. The OM fiber designations: OM1 = 62.5 micron, 200 MHz·km bandwidth; OM2 = 50 micron, 500 MHz·km; OM3 = 50 micron, 2000 MHz·km (laser-optimized); OM4 = 50 micron, 4700 MHz·km (400m at 10G). OS1/OS2 are single-mode fiber designations.

---

**Q30. Answer: C — OS1**
> OS1 (Optical Single-mode 1) is single-mode fiber designed for indoor use with a tight-buffered jacket. It supports 10 Gbps at up to 10 km. OS2 is the outdoor/loose-tube version, also single-mode, with better specifications for long-haul (up to 200+ km with amplification). OM3 and OM4 are multi-mode fiber designations. The key distinction: OS = single-mode, OM = multi-mode.

---

**Q31. Answer: D — Either 568A or 568B — same standard on both ends**
> A straight-through cable (used to connect different device types: PC to switch) simply requires the same wiring standard on BOTH ends — both 568A or both 568B. Since the patch panel uses 568B, the patch cable just needs to be 568B on both ends (or 568A on both ends — either works as a straight-through). A cable with 568A on one end and 568B on the other is a crossover cable, which is NOT appropriate for connecting a workstation to a switch port.

---

**Q32. Answer: C — Crossover cable**
> A crossover cable reverses the transmit and receive pairs — one end uses 568A, the other uses 568B. It was traditionally used to connect two similar devices (switch-to-switch, router-to-router, PC-to-PC) where both devices transmit on the same pins and receive on the same pins. Without crossing the pairs, the transmit of one device would connect to the transmit of the other — not the receive. Modern switches with Auto-MDI/X handle this automatically.

---

**Q33. Answer: C — Crossover cables**
> Auto-MDI/X (Automatic Medium-Dependent Interface Crossover) allows a switch port to automatically detect whether it is connected to a device that needs a crossover or straight-through connection, and adjust accordingly. This means you can use any patch cable (straight-through or crossover) to connect any two devices to a modern switch or router with Auto-MDI/X enabled. Crossover cables are still used in some legacy environments.

---

**Q34. Answer: D — TDR (Time Domain Reflectometer)**
> A TDR (Time Domain Reflectometer) sends a pulse down a cable and measures the time for reflections to return. Since reflections occur at impedance discontinuities (breaks, shorts, kinks), the TDR can calculate the distance to the fault based on the signal's travel time. An OTDR (Optical TDR) performs the same function for fiber optic cables. A cable toner finds cables in bundles. A multimeter tests electrical continuity.

---

**Q35. Answer: D — Toner and probe (fox and hound)**
> A toner and probe set (also called a fox and hound or tone generator and inductive amplifier) consists of two parts: the tone generator connected to one end of an unknown cable injects a signal, and the inductive probe detects that signal when held near the cable anywhere along its length. Used to trace and identify unknown cables in bundles, walls, and patch panels without requiring cable access at both ends.

---

## Multiple Answer — Q36 through Q45

---

**Q36. Answer: B — Cat6 (up to 55m), C — Cat6a (up to 100m)**
> Cat5e: max 1 Gbps at 100m — does NOT support 10 Gbps. Cat6: supports 10 Gbps but only at 55 meters maximum. Cat6a: supports 10 Gbps at the full 100-meter run. Cat7 also supports 10G at 100m but uses non-standard connectors (GG45 or TERA). Cat3 and Cat5 are far below 10G capability. For the exam: if the question asks about 10G at full distance, the answer is Cat6a.

---

**Q37. Answer: B — Longer distances, C — Smaller core/single light path, E — Used for WAN/long-haul**
> Single-mode fiber (SMF): 8–10 micron core (smaller, not larger), single light path = no modal dispersion, supports distances up to 100+ km, used for inter-campus and WAN links. SMF is actually more expensive than MMF (smaller core = more precise manufacturing, more expensive laser sources). Multi-mode fiber: 50 or 62.5 micron core (larger), multiple light paths, less expensive, used in data centers and campuses (up to ~550m for OM4 at 10G).

---

**Q38. Answer: A — Attenuation, B — Crosstalk, D — EMI**
> Copper cable signal degradation causes: (1) Attenuation = resistive signal loss over distance (heat), (2) Crosstalk = inductive coupling between adjacent wire pairs (NEXT, FEXT, alien crosstalk), (3) EMI = external electromagnetic fields from motors, generators, fluorescent lights, power cables inducing noise into unshielded conductors. Dispersion (modal or chromatic) is a fiber optic phenomenon — light pulses spreading over long distances. Wavelength division is a fiber technique (WDM), not a degradation cause.

---

**Q39. Answer: B — Immune to EMI/RFI, D — Longer distances and higher bandwidth**
> Fiber optic cables transmit light pulses, not electrical signals. Light is not affected by electromagnetic or radio frequency interference — making fiber immune to EMI/RFI. This makes fiber ideal for environments with heavy electrical equipment. Fiber also supports far greater distances (km vs. 100m for copper) and bandwidth (Tbps capacity vs. Gbps for copper). Fiber is significantly more expensive to install and terminate than copper.

---

**Q40. Answer: A — SFP (1G), C — SFP+ (10G), D — QSFP+ (40G)**
> The SFP transceiver family: SFP = 1 Gbps (mini-GBIC), SFP+ = 10 Gbps (same form factor as SFP), SFP28 = 25 Gbps, QSFP = 40 Gbps (4 × 10G), QSFP+ = 40 Gbps, QSFP28 = 100 Gbps (4 × 25G), QSFP56 = 200 Gbps, QSFP-DD = 400 Gbps. GBIC is the older, larger predecessor to SFP — it is NOT a small form-factor module. RJ-45 is a copper connector, not a transceiver.

---

**Q41. Answer: B — Required in plenum air-handling spaces, D — More expensive than PVC**
> Plenum cable (CMP) is required by the NEC (National Electrical Code) in air-handling plenum spaces. Its fluoropolymer jacket is fire-resistant and produces minimal toxic smoke. The materials (PVDF, FEP) are more expensive than standard PVC, making plenum cable 2–3× more expensive. Plenum cable does NOT provide higher data speeds — a Cat6a plenum cable has the same electrical performance as a standard Cat6a cable. It uses the same RJ-45 connectors.

---

**Q42. Answer: B — LC, D — SC, E — MPO**
> Fiber optic connectors: LC (small, latch, high-density, SFP), SC (square, push-pull, older enterprise), ST (bayonet twist-lock, legacy), MPO/MTP (multi-fiber, 40G/100G), FC (threaded, precision, test equipment/DWDM), MT-RJ (duplex, small, legacy). RJ-45 is a copper connector (8P8C) for UTP/STP Ethernet — it has nothing to do with fiber. RJ-11 is a 6P2C connector for telephone lines.

---

**Q43. Answer: B — Supports 10G at full 100m, D — Larger diameter and additional shielding**
> Cat6a (Category 6 Augmented) was specifically designed to support 10GBase-T at the full 100-meter run. Achieving this required: (1) increased cable diameter (typically 7–8mm vs. 6mm for Cat6) to physically separate the wire pairs, (2) individual pair shielding in some variants (U/FTP or F/FTP) to control alien crosstalk at 10G frequencies (500 MHz bandwidth), (3) tighter specifications for insertion loss and crosstalk. Cat6a uses the same 23-gauge conductors as Cat6 — NOT thinner conductors.

---

**Q44. Answer: B — 1000Base-SX, D — 10GBase-SR, E — 10GBase-LR**
> Fiber Ethernet standards: 100Base-FX = MMF fiber (100 Mbps), 1000Base-SX = MMF (1G, 850nm, up to 550m on OM3), 1000Base-LX = SMF or MMF (1G, 1310nm, up to 5km), 10GBase-SR = MMF (10G, 850nm), 10GBase-LR = SMF (10G, 1310nm, 10km), 10GBase-ER = SMF (10G, 1550nm, 40km). 1000Base-T and 10GBase-T are copper standards. The naming convention: S = short (MMF), L = long (SMF), E = extended (SMF, very long).

---

**Q45. Answer: B — Transceivers built into cable ends, D — Low-cost short-distance solution**
> DAC (Direct Attach Copper) cables have permanently attached SFP+, QSFP, or QSFP28 modules at both ends of a twin-axial copper cable assembly. They plug directly into switch/router SFP ports. No separate transceivers are needed (the transceivers ARE the cable ends). Maximum distances: passive DAC (up to 5–7m), active DAC (up to 15m). They are NOT fiber — they use copper conductors. They are significantly cheaper than optical transceivers + fiber for short rack-to-rack connections.

## Scenario-Based — Q46 through Q52

---

**Q46. Answer: C — Cat6a**
> Requirements: support 10 Gbps, runs up to 90 meters, budget-conscious. Analysis: Cat6 supports 10G but only at 55m — the 90m runs exceed this. Cat6a supports 10G at the full 100m and fits within 90m runs. Cat8 supports 25/40G but only at 30m and is far more expensive — overkill for 90m runs to the desktop. Single-mode fiber would work but is far more expensive to install and terminate. Cat6a is the cost-effective, standards-compliant choice.

---

**Q47. Answer: C — EMI from fluorescent lights and electrical conduit**
> The diagnostic clues: cable PASSES electrical tests (correct wiring, continuity, no opens/shorts) but has intermittent connectivity problems. The cable runs near fluorescent lights and electrical conduit — both strong EMI sources. UTP cable relies only on the twist of the pairs to reject interference — in high-EMI environments this is often insufficient. The cable is 95m (within the 100m limit). The wiring standard doesn't cause intermittent issues. The solution: replace UTP with STP or route the cable away from EMI sources.

---

**Q48. Answer: D — QSFP28 DAC cables**
> Requirements: 100 Gbps, less than 3 meters. Analysis: At 3 meters, DAC cables are the perfect cost-effective solution. QSFP28 DAC cables support 100 Gbps with integrated transceivers at a fraction of the cost of fiber transceivers + cables. Cat6a UTP maxes at 10G. Single-mode and multi-mode fiber with QSFP28 would work at this distance but cost significantly more than DAC. For data center short-reach (under 7m) high-speed connections, DAC is the industry-standard cost-effective choice.

---

**Q49. Answer: D — Use plenum-rated (CMP) Cat6**
> In air-handling plenum spaces, plenum-rated cable is not optional — it is required by building and fire codes (NEC Article 800). Standard PVC cable emits toxic smoke when burned that would be distributed throughout the building by the HVAC system. The cost difference is not justification for code violation and the associated life-safety risk. This is also a common exam scenario: when the question mentions "plenum space," the answer is always plenum-rated cable.

---

**Q50. Answer: C — Clean the fiber connectors with a proper cleaning kit**
> Contaminated fiber connectors (dust, oil, moisture) are the #1 cause of high fiber optic attenuation and link failures. The standard troubleshooting approach follows the same principle as any troubleshooting: simplest fix first. Cleaning connectors is fast, free, and solves contamination issues immediately. An OTDR can confirm the location of the attenuation after cleaning. Replacing the entire cable run or transceivers before cleaning connectors violates proper troubleshooting methodology.

---

**Q51. Answer: C — Single-mode fiber with 10GBase-LR**
> Requirements: 8 km distance, 10 Gbps. Analysis: 8 km is far beyond copper (100m max) and multi-mode fiber limits (OM3 = 300m, OM4 = 400m at 10G). Single-mode fiber is the only appropriate choice for this distance. 10GBase-LR supports 10 Gbps over single-mode fiber at 1310 nm for up to 10 km — it exactly meets the 8 km requirement with margin. 10GBase-ER (40km SMF) would also work but is more expensive.

---

**Q52. Answer: C — Crossover cable — NOT appropriate for workstation-to-switch**
> A cable with 568A on one end and 568B on the other is a CROSSOVER cable. Crossover cables were used to connect two similar devices (switch-to-switch, PC-to-PC). For a workstation connecting to a switch, a STRAIGHT-THROUGH cable is required (same wiring standard on both ends — both 568A or both 568B). Note: Most modern switches have Auto-MDI/X which would auto-correct this, but the question tests the fundamental concept. The patch panel wiring standard doesn't affect what the patch cable needs to be.

## Fill in the Blank — Q53 through Q55

---

**Q53. Answer: Single-mode / Multi-mode**
> **Single-mode** fiber uses an 8–10 micron core with a single light path, supporting very long distances (10 km to 100+ km) for WAN and inter-campus links. **Multi-mode** fiber uses a 50 or 62.5 micron core with multiple simultaneous light paths, optimized for shorter distances within data centers and campus environments (up to 400–550m for OM3/OM4 at 10G). The key exam distinction: SMF = long distance and expensive; MMF = short distance and less expensive.

---

**Q54. Answer: 568B / green-white & green / orange-white & orange**
> The **568B** wiring standard is the most common in North America for Ethernet. The difference between 568A and 568B is the position of the green and orange pairs: in 568A, pins 1-2 use the green pair and pins 3-6 use the orange pair; in 568B, these pairs are swapped — pins 1-2 use the orange pair and pins 3-6 use the green pair. A crossover cable has 568A on one end and 568B on the other, swapping these pairs to reverse transmit and receive.

---

**Q55. Answer: Attenuation / Crosstalk**
> **Attenuation** is the signal loss over distance caused by electrical resistance in copper conductors — the longer the cable, the weaker the signal arrives at the far end. The 100-meter maximum for UTP is defined by acceptable attenuation limits. **Crosstalk** is the inductive interference between adjacent wire pairs in the same cable (or between cables — alien crosstalk). NEXT (Near-End Crosstalk) is measured near the transmitter. FEXT (Far-End Crosstalk) is measured at the far end. Higher cable category ratings specify tighter limits on both attenuation and crosstalk.
